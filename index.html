TV Energy Consumption Data Story

This website presents a data story based on an Australian television energy dataset. It helps consumers understand which screen technologies are common, which brands dominate the market, and how screen size affects energy consumption.

1. Who is the audience?

The audience for this visualisation is Australian consumers who are interested in buying a television. They are likely to want practical information that helps them compare products, understand the market, and make better decisions about energy use.

2. What does the audience want to know?

  • Which screen technologies are most common?
  • Which TV brands are most common in the dataset?
  • How does screen size affect energy consumption?

3. Question 1: Which screen technologies are more frequent?

This question shows which screen technologies appear most often in the dataset. It gives the audience a general view of the types of televisions available in the market.

Answer

This chart compares the number of TVs in each screen technology category, such as LCD, LCD (LED), and OLED. It shows which technologies are most common in the dataset and which are less common. This gives useful market context before looking at brands and energy consumption.

Main takeaway: Screen technologies are not represented equally, so some technologies are much more common than others in the dataset.

4. Question 2: Which TV brands are most common?

This question looks at how TV models are distributed across brands. It helps show whether the market is dominated by a few major brands or shared more evenly across many brands.

Answer

This pie chart shows that Samsung is the largest named brand in the dataset. However, the "Other" category is even larger, which means many smaller brands together make up a major share of the market. Kogan and LG also have noticeable shares, while Hisense and Eko contribute smaller proportions.

This suggests that the TV market is made up of both strong major brands and a large number of smaller competing brands. In other words, Samsung is the most dominant individual brand, but the market overall is still quite diverse.

Main takeaway: Samsung is the leading individual brand, but the large "Other" category shows that many smaller brands together make up a very significant part of the market.

5. Question 3: How does screen size affect energy consumption?

This question examines the relationship between television screen size and labelled energy consumption. A scatter plot is used because it is well suited to showing how two numeric variables are related.

Answer

This scatter plot shows a clear positive relationship between screen size and energy consumption. As screen size increases, labelled energy consumption also increases. Smaller televisions tend to use less power, while larger televisions use much more.

Although there is some spread in the data, the overall pattern is very strong. This means that screen size is a major factor affecting how much energy a television uses.

Main takeaway: The bigger the screen size, the higher the energy consumption.

6. Final Conclusion

This data story shows three important insights. First, some screen technologies are more common than others in the dataset. Second, Samsung is the most dominant individual brand, but many smaller brands together make up a very large part of the market. Third, and most importantly, energy consumption increases as screen size increases.

For consumers, this means that brand and technology matter, but screen size is one of the most important factors to consider when thinking about energy use. Choosing a larger TV is likely to result in higher electricity consumption.

7. About the Data

Data source

The dataset is based on television energy information available for the Australian market.

Data processing

The data was cleaned and transformed in KNIME. This included filtering columns, cleaning text values, grouping records, and preparing tables for visualisation.

Privacy

This dataset contains product information rather than personal information, so privacy concerns are minimal.

Accuracy and limitations

The dataset reflects the version downloaded for this exercise and may change over time. Some results may also be influenced by cleaning decisions and grouping choices made during preprocessing.

Ethics

The visualisation aims to present the data honestly and clearly. It is important not to exaggerate conclusions or suggest claims that are not supported by the dataset.

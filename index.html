TV Energy Consumption Data Story

This website presents a data story based on an Australian television energy consumption dataset. The aim is to help everyday consumers understand how screen technology, screen size, and energy use relate to each other.

1. Who is the audience?

The audience for this visualisation is Australian consumers who are interested in buying a television. This audience is likely to want simple and practical information rather than technical detail. They want to know which TVs are common, which types are usually larger, and what factors affect energy consumption.

Visualisation guidelines

  • Use simple language and clear chart titles.
  • Present one main idea in each section.
  • Explain what the chart shows, not just display it.
  • Avoid misleading conclusions by adding context.

2. What does the audience want to know?

The audience is likely to ask these questions:

  • Which screen technologies are most common?
  • What is the average screen size of each screen technology?
  • How does energy consumption change with screen size and technology?

3. Question 1: Which screen technologies are more frequent?

This question helps show which types of TVs appear most often in the dataset. It gives the audience context about the market and helps them understand whether some technologies are more common than others.

Answer

This chart shows the number of TVs for each screen technology category, such as LCD, LCD (LED), and OLED. It allows the audience to quickly compare which technologies appear most often in the dataset. A more common technology may give consumers more product choices in the market.

Main takeaway: This chart provides market context by showing which TV screen technologies are most frequently represented.

4. Question 2: What is the average screen size of each type?

This question helps explain whether some screen technologies are usually sold in larger sizes than others. This is important because screen size can affect both price and energy use.

Answer

This chart compares the average screen size of each screen technology. It shows whether technologies such as OLED are generally associated with larger TVs than LCD or LCD (LED). This is important because a larger average screen size may help explain why some technologies appear to use more energy.

Main takeaway: Screen technology and screen size are related, so size must be considered before making energy comparisons.

5. Question 3: How does energy consumption vary by screen size and technology?

This is the main data story question. It compares average energy consumption across small, medium, and large TVs for each screen technology.

Answer

This graph shows the average energy consumption of TVs based on both screen size and screen technology. The clearest pattern is that energy consumption increases as screen size increases. Large TVs have the highest average energy consumption, medium TVs are lower, and small TVs use the least energy.

The graph also shows some differences between LCD, LCD (LED), and OLED. However, the effect of screen size is stronger than the effect of screen technology. For example, all large TVs use much more energy than small TVs, regardless of the technology used.

OLED and LCD (LED) televisions often appear to use more energy than LCD televisions, but this should be interpreted carefully. Earlier results showed that some technologies are associated with larger average screen sizes, which means higher energy use may be partly explained by size rather than technology alone.

Main takeaway: Screen size is the main factor influencing energy consumption. Screen technology matters, but it should not be compared without considering size.

6. Final conclusion

The data story shows that screen size has a major impact on TV energy consumption. Although there are differences between screen technologies, these differences cannot be interpreted fairly without also considering the size of the television.

For consumers, this means that choosing a smaller TV may have a bigger effect on energy use than simply choosing a different screen technology. A useful buying decision should therefore consider both the technology and the screen size together.

7. About the data

Data source

The dataset is based on television energy information available for the Australian market.

Data processing

The data was cleaned and transformed in KNIME. This included filtering columns, cleaning text values, grouping records, creating screen-size categories, and summarising data for charts.

Privacy

This dataset contains product information, not personal information, so privacy risk is low.

Accuracy and limitations

The dataset represents the version downloaded for this exercise and may change over time. Results may also be influenced by category definitions, cleaning decisions, and the structure of the available data.

Ethics

The visualisation aims to present the data honestly and avoid misleading claims. It is important not to suggest that one screen technology is always worse without acknowledging the influence of screen size.
